# Gel Chemistry

## Monomers/Oligomers

Gel is a pre-polymer system. The liquid gel contains **monomers** (tiny reactive molecules, primarily acrylates and methacrylates like HEMA) and **oligomers** (short pre-assembled chains that give the gel its thickness and viscosity). When cured, these link together into a 3D crosslinked **polymer** network. That's why the finish is hard and chip-resistant.

Key distinction worth knowing:

- **Hard gels** are densely crosslinked: they have to be filed off because acetone can't penetrate the network.
- **Soft / soak-off gels** are more loosely crosslinked: acetone can swell and disrupt the network, which is what makes them removable by soaking.

## Photoinitiators

Photoinitiators are light-sensitive compounds that absorb photons at specific wavelengths and break apart into **free radicals** (highly unstable atoms with unpaired electrons). Those free radicals then attack the double bonds in the acrylate monomers, kicking off the polymerization chain reaction.

Common photoinitiators include benzophenone, camphorquinone, and TPO. They're typically present at 1–5% by weight in a formula.

Each photoinitiator only responds to light within its specific **absorption band** — a narrow range of wavelengths. If your lamp doesn't emit light in that range at sufficient intensity, the photoinitiators don't activate properly, and the gel doesn't cure properly. This is the entire reason lamp-to-formula compatibility matters so much.

## Polymerization

The curing process has three phases:

1. **Initiation**: photoinitiator absorbs a photon and breaks into free radicals
2. **Propagation**: each radical attacks a monomer's double bond. It becomes a new radical and it attacks the next monomer; the chain goes down rapidly.
3. **Termination**: two radicals combine or disproportionate, ending the chain.

The outermost surface of a curing gel layer is exposed to oxygen, which scavenges free radicals and stops the reaction at the very surface. This is what creates the **inhibition layer**. It's not a defect or a sign of undercure and only affects the outermost few microns.

Polymerization is also **exothermic**. This is why you can sometimes feel a heat spike when your nails are in the lamp.

For a bit more detail into the termination, picture two polymer chains, each with an unpaired electron on the end. When two radicals combine, they bump into each other, and the two unpaired electrons pair up and form a bond between the chains. The two chains essentially fuse into one longer chain, and neither end is reactive anymore. When they disproportionate, one chain steals a hydrogen atom from the other. The chain that lost the hydrogen ends up with a double bond at its tip and becomes stable. The chain that gained the hydrogen also becomes stable, but the chains stay separate rather than merging.

These termination events, combined with oxygen stealing free radicala at the surface, are part of why 100% monomer conversion never happens. There are always some chains that terminate early, leaving a small fraction of monomers that never got incorporated into the network.

## Ingredients

**HEMA (2-hydroxyethyl methacrylate)** is the most common monomer in traditional gel formulas. It's also the most studied and documented sensitizer in gel nail chemistry. Other common monomers include HPMA, EGDMA, TREGDA, and urethane acrylates.

**MMA (methyl methacrylate)** is a separate molecule. The FDA has recommended against its use in nail products since 1970 due to severe sensitization risk, including neuropathy and permanent nail loss. Reputable brands don't use it. A strong solvent-like or nail-glue smell from a gel that also won't soak off with acetone is a warning sign.

The trend toward **HEMA-free** formulas replaces free HEMA monomers with pre-reacted polymers or alternative monomers. These can be genuinely lower-risk, but "HEMA-free" does not mean "acrylate-free" and can still cause reactions in sensitized individuals.
